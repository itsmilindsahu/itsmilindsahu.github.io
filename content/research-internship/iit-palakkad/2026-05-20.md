## What I worked on this week

This week I read *A Gaussian Process Regression Model for Distribution Inputs* by Bachoc et al. The main idea I focused on was how Wasserstein distance can be used to compare entire probability distributions, rather than reducing them to just their means or a few summary statistics.

This connected well with the research direction I am trying to build: using spatial statistics to understand where digital banking adoption is low and why.

## Key learning

The Gaussian process paper helped me understand that the choice of similarity measure is very important. In ordinary spatial Kriging, similarity usually comes from geographic distance. But in the Bachoc et al. paper, the inputs themselves are distributions, so the similarity between two inputs is measured using Wasserstein distance.

This made me think more carefully about my own project. Two pin codes may have similar average adoption rates but very different underlying user behaviour. Wasserstein distance might help capture those differences better than a mean-based comparison.

## Related work I went through

I also spent time going through the basic ideas behind:

- Moran’s I for testing whether adoption is spatially clustered
- Local Moran’s I / LISA for identifying specific high-high, low-low, and outlier regions
- Kriging and variograms for predicting adoption in locations where direct data is unavailable
- Difference-in-differences as a possible way to estimate campaign impact

## Research proposal

I presented my research proposal this week. The proposal is about studying digital banking adoption across localities using synthetic Pune-like pin-code data. The core plan is to combine Moran’s I, Kriging, Wasserstein distance, and a rough DiD setup.

The part I am most confident about is the spatial statistics side: Moran’s I and Kriging. The part I am still unsure about is whether Wasserstein distance can be used meaningfully for control-zone selection in a causal design. I raised this as an open question in the proposal.
